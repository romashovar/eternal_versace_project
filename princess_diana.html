Princess Diana Versace Dress, 1997

1 / 3

In 1997, Princess Diana wore a beautiful Versace dress for the cover of Harper’s Bazaar magazine. The photos became very famous because they were taken shortly before she passed away and showed her elegant modern style.

In 1997, Diana, Princess of Wales appeared on the cover of Harper’s Bazaar wearing a sleek dress designed by Versace, photographed by Mario Testino shortly before her death the same year. The shoot marked an important moment in Diana’s post-royal style transformation, as she increasingly collaborated with leading fashion houses and adopted a more modern, minimalist aesthetic. The Versace designs she wore during this period symbolized her close friendship with Gianni Versace and her growing role as a global fashion icon beyond royal protocol. After both Diana and Gianni Versace died in 1997, the images gained additional historical resonance, often interpreted as part of the final visual record of two influential cultural figures. Today, the cover is considered a defining document of late-1990s fashion and celebrity imagery.

The 1997 Harper’s Bazaar editorial featuring Diana, Princess of Wales in Versace represents a historically charged convergence of late-twentieth-century celebrity culture, editorial fashion photography, and the final phase of Diana’s carefully constructed post-royal sartorial identity. Produced in collaboration with photographer Mario Testino and styled largely in garments by Versace—a house with which Diana maintained a personal friendship through Gianni Versace—the shoot articulated a refined minimalist glamour that diverged sharply from the ceremonial dressing codes of her earlier royal years. The editorial’s publication shortly before the deaths of both Diana (August 1997) and Gianni Versace (July 1997) imbued the imagery with retrospective memorial significance, transforming the photographs into symbolic artifacts of a pivotal cultural moment. Within fashion historiography, the cover is frequently cited as an example of how editorial imagery can function simultaneously as personal image-making, luxury-brand association, and historical documentation, encapsulating the late-1990s shift toward globally mediated celebrity fashion narratives.
